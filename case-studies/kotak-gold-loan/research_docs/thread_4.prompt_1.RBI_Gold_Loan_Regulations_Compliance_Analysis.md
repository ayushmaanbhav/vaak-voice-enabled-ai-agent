## Executive Summary

The Indian gold loan industry is undergoing a significant regulatory transformation. The RBI has intensified oversight following multiple compliance failures by major NBFCs, most notably the **IIFL Finance gold loan ban in March 2024**. The September 2024 RBI circular marked a watershed moment, identifying widespread irregularities and mandating comprehensive compliance overhauls within 3 months.

### Key Findings

| Aspect | Summary |
|--------|---------|
| **Market Size** | ₹7.1 lakh crore (2024), expected to reach ₹14.19 lakh crore by 2028 |
| **Bank Gold Loan Growth** | 50-68% YoY growth in 2024, significantly outpacing NBFC growth |
| **NBFC Market Share** | ~60% but facing regulatory pressure |
| **Unorganized Sector** | 65% of market - massive opportunity for organized players |
| **Major Regulatory Action** | IIFL Finance banned from gold loans (March-September 2024) |
| **New Guidelines** | Effective April 2026 with comprehensive reforms |

### The Regulatory Shift Creates Opportunity

The tightening regulatory environment creates a **first-mover advantage for banks** with strong compliance frameworks like Kotak Mahindra Bank. Legacy NBFCs face significant costs to upgrade systems, while banks can leverage existing infrastructure for faster, compliant growth.

## RBI Regulatory Framework for Gold Loans

### 1. Core Regulatory Guidelines

#### 1.1 Loan-to-Value (LTV) Ratio Norms

The RBI has introduced a **tiered LTV structure** effective June 2025:

| Loan Amount | Maximum LTV |
|-------------|-------------|
| Up to ₹2.5 lakh | 85% |
| ₹2.5 lakh - ₹5 lakh | 80% |
| Above ₹5 lakh | 75% |

**Key Requirements:**
- LTV must be maintained throughout the loan tenure (ongoing basis)
- For bullet repayment loans, accrued interest is included in LTV calculation
- Valuation must use the **lower of**:
  - 30-day average closing price
  - Previous day's closing price
- Price source: India Bullion and Jewellers Association (IBJA) or SEBI-regulated commodity exchange
- Collateral benchmarked to 22 carats; lower purity gold converted to 22-carat equivalent

**Historical Context:**
- COVID-era temporary increase to 90% LTV expired in March 2021
- Standard 75% cap was maintained until June 2025 tiered introduction

#### 1.2 Interest Rate Regulations

- **No direct cap on interest rates** but subject to fair lending code
- NBFCs must follow "Fair Practices Code" issued by RBI
- Interest rate calculation methodology must be disclosed upfront
- Penal charges must be reasonable and disclosed
- Prepayment penalties are prohibited for floating rate loans

#### 1.3 Cash Disbursement Limits

| Aspect | Requirement |
|--------|-------------|
| Maximum cash disbursal | ₹20,000 per loan |
| Rationale | Income Tax Act compliance; anti-money laundering |
| Enforcement | Violations attract supervisory action |

**May 2024 Warning:** RBI specifically cautioned Muthoot Finance and Manappuram Finance about cash disbursal violations, causing 9% stock price drops.

#### 1.4 Eligible Collateral

**Accepted:**
- Gold ornaments and jewellery
- Specially minted coins (22 carats or higher) sold by banks
- Maximum 50 grams in coins
- Maximum 1 kg total gold pledge

**Not Accepted:**
- Primary gold/silver bullion
- Gold ETFs or mutual fund units
- Financial assets backed by gold/silver

#### 1.5 Loan Tenure Restrictions

| Loan Type | Maximum Tenure |
|-----------|----------------|
| Bullet repayment (consumption) | 12 months |
| EMI-based loans | Up to 4 years (bank-dependent) |
| Agricultural gold loans | Generally up to 12 months |

**Renewal Conditions:**
- Renewal only permitted if borrower clears due interest
- Rolling over with only partial payments flagged as irregular practice

### 2. Licensing & Registration Requirements

#### 2.1 For NBFCs

There is **no separate "gold loan license"** from RBI. Requirements include:

1. **Valid NBFC Registration** under RBI Act, 1934
2. **Minimum Net Owned Fund (NOF):** ₹10 crore for NBFC-ICC
3. **Scale-Based Regulation (SBR):** Classification as Base Layer, Middle Layer, Upper Layer, or Top Layer NBFC
4. **Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR):** Minimum 15% for all NBFCs
5. **Trained loan officers** in RBI gold loan documentation and KYC norms
6. **Infrastructure requirements:** Vaults, assaying equipment, security systems

#### 2.2 Banks vs. NBFCs - Regulatory Differences

| Aspect | Banks | NBFCs |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Regulator | RBI (full) | RBI (primary) |
| LTV Cap | 75% (now tiered) | Same as banks |
| Priority Sector Lending | Agricultural gold loans count toward PSL | Not applicable |
| Deposit Taking | Allowed | Only NBFC-D with specific license |
| Capital Adequacy | 9% (Basel III) | 15% |
| Risk Weight (Gold Loans) | 125% after mitigation | 125% |
| Supervision Intensity | Higher | Relatively lower (changing) |
| CRR/SLR Requirements | Applicable | Not applicable |

### 3. KYC Requirements

**Standard Requirements:**
- Valid photo ID (Aadhaar, PAN, Passport, Voter ID, Driving License)
- Address proof
- PAN card mandatory for loans above ₹5 lakh
- Proof of gold ownership (original receipt or declaration for family gold)
- Customer presence during gold valuation

**2022 Update:** RBI permitted digital/video KYC for gold loans, enabling online verification

**2024 Enhancement:** November 2024 KYC amendments for increased transparency and stricter customer verification

## Security Rules & Compliance Requirements

### 1. Vault Security Standards

| Requirement | Specification |
|-------------|--------------|
| Storage Location | Secured vaults on lender's premises only |
| Infrastructure | Standardized assaying equipment at every lending branch |
| Inventory Tracking | Digital system with unique item identification |
| Storage Containers | Sealed with tamper-evident features |
| Security Measures | Adequate infrastructure and security at every branch |

**Post-IIFL Tightening (2024):**
- Enhanced physical security requirements
- Mandatory CCTV surveillance
- Biometric access controls
- Dual custody requirements for vault access

### 2. Insurance Requirements

| Coverage | Requirement |
|----------|-------------|
| Type | Comprehensive insurance against theft, fire, natural disasters |
| Scope | All pledged gold in vaults |
| Beneficiary | Both lender and borrower protected |
| Mandatory | Yes, for all NBFCs and banks |

### 3. Audit Requirements

| Audit Type | Frequency | Requirement |
|------------|-----------|-------------|
| Internal Stock Audit | Quarterly | Mandatory |
| External Audit | Annual | Mandatory |
| Surprise Verification | Periodic | Mandatory |
| RBI Inspection | As scheduled | Mandatory cooperation |

**Discrepancy Protocol:**
1. Immediate investigation
2. Borrower notification
3. Management escalation
4. Regulatory reporting

### 4. Gold Valuation Standards

| Aspect | Requirement |
|--------|-------------|
| Customer Presence | Mandatory during valuation |
| Purity Testing | BIS-certified or standardized assaying |
| Weight Recording | Both gross and net weight documented |
| Certificate Generation | Digital certificates with unique tracking |
| Valuer Qualifications | Trained, certified personnel |

### 5. Auction Process Rules

**Pre-Auction Requirements:**
- Minimum notice period to borrower before auction
- Public announcement in at least 2 newspapers (1 regional, 1 national)
- Fair reserve price based on current gold value
- Customer consent for auction terms in loan agreement

**Post-Auction Requirements:**
- Surplus refund within 7 working days
- Transparent disclosure of auction proceeds
- Documentation of auction process

**Customer Protection (New 2025 Rules):**
- ₹5,000 per day penalty for delay in returning gold beyond 7 working days after loan repayment
- Same penalty for delay in surplus refund

### 6. Operational Compliance

**Loan Agreement Must Include:**
- Description of gold collateral
- Value of collateral
- Auction procedure details
- Circumstances leading to auction
- Notice period for repayment before auction
- Timelines for gold release post-repayment
- Surplus refund process

**Record Keeping:**
- Complete loan documentation
- Customer consent records
- Valuation certificates
- Transaction history
- Audit trail for all gold movements

### The September 2024 Watershed Circular

**Date:** September 30, 2024

**Scope:** All commercial banks (including SFBs), primary urban co-operative banks, and NBFCs (excluding RRBs and payment banks)

**Key Deficiencies Identified:**

1. **Third-Party/Fintech Issues:**
   - Gold valuation in customer's absence
   - Credit appraisal by fintech/BC instead of lender
   - Gold stored in fintech/BC custody
   - Delayed/insecure gold transportation to branches

2. **LTV Violations:**
   - Weakness in ongoing LTV monitoring
   - Incorrect calculation of LTV
   - Failure to maintain LTV throughout tenure

3. **Transparency Issues:**
   - Lack of transparency in gold auctions
   - Auction values lower than estimated gold worth
   - Non-disclosure of charges to customers

4. **Operational Irregularities:**
   - Rolling over loans with partial payments
   - Non-categorization of gold loans as NPAs despite default
   - High volume of loans to same PAN without adequate monitoring
   - Top-up loans without fresh valuation
   - Missing unique identifiers for top-up loans in CBS/LPS

5. **Cash Handling:**
   - Cash disbursals exceeding ₹20,000 statutory limit
   - Inadequate documentation of cash transactions

6. **Risk Weight Misapplication:**
   - Incorrect application of risk weights
   - Capital adequacy understatement

**Deadline:** December 30, 2024 for compliance report to RBI Senior Supervisory Manager

**Consequence:** Non-compliance attracts supervisory action

### 2025 Draft and Final Directions

**April 2025 Draft Key Proposals:**
- Harmonized framework across banks, NBFCs, cooperative banks, HFCs
- Tiered LTV structure
- Bullet loan tenure cap at 12 months
- Enhanced customer protection measures
- Standardized auction procedures

**June 2025 Final Directions:**
- All proposals confirmed
- Implementation date: April 1, 2026
- Existing loans grandfathered until maturity
- Comprehensive borrower protection framework

### Digital Lending Guidelines Applicability

The RBI's 2022 Digital Lending Guidelines apply to gold loans when:
- Loans sourced through digital platforms
- Physical interface may exist but digital technology is "largely" used
- Fintech partnerships for sourcing/servicing

**Key Requirements:**
- Transparency in pricing
- Borrower consent for data use
- Grievance redressal mechanism
- Restriction on third-party access to customer data

## Major Industry Failures & Blunders

### 1. IIFL Finance - Gold Loan Ban (March-September 2024)

**THE DEFINING CASE STUDY OF THE ERA**

#### Background
IIFL Finance was among the top 2 NBFCs in the gold loan business with:
- Gold loan portfolio: ₹24,692 crore (Q3 FY24)
- Gold loans as % of total AUM: 32%
- Total loan book: ₹77,444 crore

#### RBI Action (March 4, 2024)
The RBI banned IIFL Finance from:
- Sanctioning new gold loans
- Disbursing new gold loans
- Assigning/securitizing/selling gold loans

**Effective:** Immediate

#### Violations Identified

| Violation Category | Specific Issues |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Gold Purity Assessment** | Serious deviations in assaying and certifying purity at sanction and auction |
| **Net Weight Certification** | Incorrect net weight documentation |
| **LTV Ratio** | Breaches in loan-to-value ratio compliance |
| **Cash Transactions** | Significant disbursal and collection in cash far exceeding ₹20,000 limit |
| **Auction Process** | Non-adherence to standard auction procedures |
| **Transparency** | Lack of transparency in charges levied to customer accounts |

#### RBI's Strong Statement

> "These practices, apart from being regulatory violations, also significantly and adversely impact the interest of the customers... the regulator had been engaging with the senior management and the statutory auditors of the company on these deficiencies. However, no meaningful corrective action has been evidenced so far."

#### Special Audit & Resolution

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| March 4, 2024 | Ban announced |
| April 23, 2024 | Special audit by independent agency commenced |
| August 5, 2024 | Gold loan AUM declined from ₹26,081 cr to ₹12,162 cr (53% drop) |
| September 19, 2024 | RBI lifted restrictions after remedial actions |

#### Impact Analysis

| Metric | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| **Stock Price** | Significant crash on announcement |
| **AUM Erosion** | 53% decline in gold loan book |
| **Reputation** | Severe damage to brand credibility |
| **Customer Loss** | Customers migrated to competitors |
| **Industry Effect** | Triggered sector-wide scrutiny |

#### Lessons Learned

1. **Proactive compliance** is non-negotiable
2. **Senior management engagement** with RBI must result in action
3. **Statutory auditors** have responsibility to flag issues
4. **Customer interest** is paramount in RBI's supervisory approach
5. **No NBFC is "too big to regulate"**

## Advantages for New Movers

### 1. Clean Slate Compliance

**Opportunity:** Build compliance-first infrastructure without legacy burden

| Legacy Player Challenge | New Mover Advantage |
|------------------------|---------------------|
| Retrofitting old systems to new norms | Design systems for 2026 compliance from day one |
| Retraining large workforce | Hire and train with new protocols |
| Changing established processes | Implement best practices natively |
| Multiple regulatory observations on record | Clean regulatory track record |
| Customer trust damaged by past issues | Fresh brand positioning |

### 2. Technology-Native Operations

**Banks entering gold loans can leverage:**

- Existing core banking systems (CBS) already integrated with LTV monitoring
- Digital KYC infrastructure from other loan products
- Automated risk-weight calculation engines
- Real-time regulatory reporting capabilities
- Mobile and internet banking for customer service

**Cost Advantage:** NBFCs must invest heavily in new systems; banks can extend existing platforms

### 3. Lower Risk Weight Benefits

| Lender Type | External Rating | Risk Weight |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Banks (direct lending) | N/A | 125% (post-mitigation) |
| NBFCs | A and above | 125% (post-mitigation) |
| Bank loans to NBFC | A and above | Reduced by 25% in Feb 2025 |

**Implication:** Banks can lend directly with lower capital cost than funding NBFCs

### 4. Priority Sector Lending (PSL) Advantage

| Aspect | Banks | NBFCs |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Agricultural gold loans | Count toward PSL targets | No PSL benefit |
| Sub-targets | Can fulfill "Agriculture" sub-target | N/A |
| Regulatory incentive | Yes | No |

**Strategic Value:** Banks can grow gold loan book while meeting mandatory PSL requirements

### 5. Customer Trust Post-IIFL Crisis

**Post-IIFL, InCred Research Recommended:**
> "Prefer banks such as SBI, HDFC Bank over gold finance NBFCs"

**Customer Perception Shift:**
- Banks seen as more secure for gold storage
- Regulatory action reinforces NBFC risk perception
- Existing banking relationship = trust foundation
- Deposit insurance perception extends to security

### 6. Interest Rate Competitiveness

| Player | Typical Interest Rate |
|--------|----------------------|
| NBFCs (Muthoot/Manappuram) | 12-26% per annum |
| Banks (SBI/HDFC/Kotak) | 9-15% per annum |

**Advantage:** Banks can offer 3-10% lower rates due to:
- Lower cost of funds (deposits vs. borrowings)
- No CRR/SLR but still better funding costs for banks
- Established treasury operations

### 7. Existing Branch Network Leverage

**Banks entering gold loans can use:**
- Existing branch network (no new real estate needed)
- Existing vault infrastructure (upgrade vs. build)
- Established security protocols
- Trained staff (upskill vs. new hire)

**SBI Example:** 41.66% YoY growth in gold loans leveraging its 22,000+ branch network

### 8. Cross-Sell Opportunities

| Existing Bank Product | Gold Loan Cross-Sell Opportunity |
|----------------------|----------------------------------|
| Savings Account | Emergency liquidity positioning |
| Current Account | Business working capital |
| Credit Card | Lower-cost alternative marketing |
| Personal Loan | Secured loan upgrade |
| MSME Loans | Collateral supplementation |

### 9. Regulatory Relationship Advantage

- Banks have established regulatory relationships
- More frequent interaction with RBI supervisors
- Better understanding of regulatory expectations
- Proactive compliance culture
